# Avatar Deep Dive Research

## Pain Points
- "Ugh i hate rosacea!!! This is what my face looks like even on the best days! U seriously wanna cry. I feel so sad when i look in the mirror."
Insight: Even on her best days, she cannot escape the emotional weight of looking at herself. The pain is not occasional — it is daily and relentless, tied directly to her self-image.
- "I struggle to even look in the mirror anymore and hate how good my phone camera is because it picks up everything."
Insight: Technology has become an enemy. The high-resolution camera has turned an ordinary moment (checking her appearance) into a source of distress. She cannot escape her skin even in private.
- "laughing but dying at the same time. why do i torture myself?"
Insight: She is self-aware enough to know her behavior is compulsive and self-destructive, but she cannot stop it. This is the hallmark of someone trapped between self-awareness and helplessness.
- "I've been struggling with skin problems for quite a long time and developed a very bad habit. I'm 33 and I started skin picking for almost 10 years ago because I just can't get rid of clogged pores."
Insight: The emotional pain has manifested into a physical, compulsive behavior. This is no longer just a skincare problem — it is a mental health adjacent issue. The pores are the trigger, and the picking is the symptom of years of unresolved frustration.
- "I can barely wear makeup without accentuating my textured skin, and I've worn bangs to try and cover it for as long as I can remember."
Insight: She has restructured her physical appearance and lifestyle around hiding her skin. The problem has colonized her choices in ways that feel invisible to others but are constant for her.
- "I feel like I have literally tried every single skin care or makeup item that could help, and barely anything did."
Insight: CORE INSIGHT — exhaustion and diminishing hope. She is not lazy or uninformed. She has done the research, spent the money, and still feels like she has failed. This sets up the deep distrust of new promises.
- "I'm 28 now. I think a lot of the insecurity I have is post trauma. I've come a long way but still aim for better results."
Insight: The emotional wound runs deeper than current skin condition. Past acne has left psychological scars that persist even after the skin physically cleared. She carries her history on her face — literally and emotionally.

Insight: Simple, affordable wins feel almost miraculous to this avatar. When something works, it becomes near-religious — a "holy grail" they evangelize to others. The bar for joy is low because disappointment has been so frequent.
- "Mandelic acid does so well with large pores... When I use this the next day my skin is smooth, shiny and glowing. A significant difference and i have oily, very large pores, textured."
Insight: The language — "smooth, shiny and glowing" — reveals what she is truly craving: not just absence of pores, but a luminous, healthy appearance that signals vitality. Glow is the emotional proxy for feeling well in her skin.

Insight: She is willing to endure significant discomfort and downtime for even partial improvement. Victory doesn't need to be total — partial wins are celebrated. This reveals she has recalibrated her expectations downward over time.

Insight: Post-microneedling recovery language reveals what she truly wants — not just visual improvement, but tactile proof her skin is healthy. "Tighter" and "retaining moisture" are proxy signals of a skin barrier that is finally working.

## Failures
- "I use it every day and have been doing for years 🥲"
Insight: Years of faithful product use with no result. This is the avatar's defining emotional wound — effort without reward. The crying emoji says everything about how personally she takes this.
- "I use 15% and there is no real difference to be honest. Depends on your skin I guess. Couldn't find a single product that works on pores just yet."
Insight: She has tried clinical-grade percentages and still found nothing. She has started to attribute failure to her specific skin rather than the products — internalizing the failure, which deepens hopelessness.
- "I've used lots of skin care products, tried so many random things and none of them work, they all happen to break me out."
Insight: CORE INSIGHT — breaking out from products meant to help is an especially cruel failure. It creates a trust collapse around skincare products in general. She now approaches new products with defensive skepticism.
- "I have been on Isotretinoin years ago for acne and it did wonders for my skin but i'm just scared to take it again. It made me super depressed."
Insight: Her most effective past treatment came with severe psychological side effects. She carries this as a trauma. It creates a framework where "what works" and "what is safe" feel mutually exclusive — a devastating belief for someone seeking help.
- "La roche posay cicaplast baume, it clogged them even worse. Now I'm just trying to use non-comedogenic products and occasionally pick them 😐"
Insight: Even trusted, derm-recommended products have failed her. This has eroded faith in brand reputation and expert endorsement. She now relies on her own trial and error — exhausting, expensive, and emotionally draining.
- "microneedling made it worse for me."
Insight: Invasive procedures — her "last resort" options — can backfire. This fear of making things worse by doing more is a major psychological barrier to taking action on new solutions.
- "the ordinary niacinamide gave me a breakout of tiny whiteheads that looked like a rash, all over my face."
Insight: Even cult-favorite, community-recommended products have betrayed her. Every failure makes the next product attempt feel higher-stakes and more emotionally costly.

Insight: She has developed a nuanced, compromise belief: permanent pore shrinkage is impossible, but temporary improvement is real. She is shopping for management, not miracles — even if part of her still secretly hopes for the miracle.

Insight: She struggles to maintain consistency with treatments, which creates a cycle of starting, stopping, and never seeing results. Any solution must be achievable within an inconsistent, real-world routine — not a clinical-trial-level protocol.
- "It made me super depressed."
Insight: The fear of psychiatric side effects from treatments like Accutane is a hard stop for many avatars. Emotional safety is a non-negotiable. A solution that cannot credibly address this fear will be rejected before it's considered.
- "it's too expensive where I live."
Insight: CORE INSIGHT — price is a genuine barrier, not just a negotiation tactic. She has spent significant money already on things that didn't work, leaving her both financially stretched and emotionally reluctant to invest again. ROI must be legible and fast enough to feel real.
- "I'm scared to take it again."
Insight: Past negative experiences with the most effective treatments have created lasting fear. She self-protective now. She needs reassurance, not just information — someone who acknowledges what she has been through before asking her to try again.
- "I've tried other serums that made my skin worse (and I'm not talking about purging) and now I don't know if I trust it."
Insight: She has lost baseline trust in her ability to choose correctly. Decision paralysis is real. She needs someone else to carry some of the risk — a trial, a guarantee, or a community of people like her who have already succeeded.

Insight: Dietary interventions that require eliminating major food groups are non-starters for her practical life. Solutions must work within the life she already has, not demand a wholesale identity overhaul alongside the skincare changes.

## Level of Awareness
**Level: Solution Aware (transitioning toward Product Aware)**
She knows exactly what her problem is (large pores, rough texture, redness, dehydrated skin). She has already tried multiple categories of solution: topicals (niacinamide, AHAs, BHAs, retinoids, azelaic acid), procedures (microneedling, laser, chemical peels, dermabrasion), dietary changes, and prescription medications. She is actively evaluating specific products and procedures — naming brands, percentages, and application methods — which places her firmly in Solution Aware, approaching Product Aware territory.
- "I'm looking into The Ordinary products, Stratia, and Krave Beauty. Are there any products recommended to help with these issues?"
Insight: She is category-literate and brand-aware. She is not asking "what is retinol?" She is asking "which retinol, how much, and in what order." She needs conviction, not education.
- "Will Dr. Dennis Gross extra strength peel pads help? My AM routine is azelaic acid 15%, topicals faded, caudalie vinoperfect serum, moisturizer, sunscreen. My PM routine is tretinoin 0.1 every other night, & BHA the nights I don't use it."
Insight: This is a highly informed consumer who has a sophisticated existing routine. She is evaluating whether a specific product adds value on top of what she is already doing — a Product Aware level question dressed in Solution Aware clothing.
- "I've tried literally everything to 'fix' my skin but I realized that some people just win the genetic lottery with their skin."
Insight: Her awareness is cycling back toward resignation — the risk of the "Most Aware but burned out" state where she knows every solution but trusts none of them. The entry point must be empathy before pitch.

## Real Customer Language
- "orange peel skin" — Why this phrase matters: This is the precise, visceral metaphor she uses to describe the dimensional, dimpled texture she sees. It's not "rough skin" — it's a specific visual that signals she has studied her skin closely enough to map it onto a recognizable object. Any copy that uses this term will signal immediate recognition.
- "glass skin" — Why this phrase matters: This is her aspirational north star. It's borrowed from Korean beauty culture and represents skin so smooth, clear, and luminous it appears translucent. She knows it may be unattainable but reaches for it anyway. Copy that acknowledges this term — and the tension between desire and reality — will earn her trust.
- "huge pores" / "large pores" — Why this phrase matters: CORE INSIGHT — this is the most repeated phrase across all posts. It is her primary self-diagnosis. She does not say "dilated follicles" or "sebaceous hyperplasia" — she says "huge pores." Mirror her language exactly.
- "skin texture" — Why this phrase matters: CORE INSIGHT — the second most repeated phrase. Used broadly to describe everything from bumpiness to roughness to unevenness. It is her umbrella term for the problem. Any solution that does not address "texture" specifically will not feel relevant to her.
- "holy grail" — Why this phrase matters: This is the language she uses for products that have genuinely transformed her skin. It signals religious-level devotion. If a product earns this label in her community, it is nearly immune to competition. Products that aspire to this status must perform visibly and consistently.
- "sandwich method" / "purging" / "skin barrier" — Why these phrases matter: She speaks the language of the skincare-educated consumer fluently. Using clinical or lay terminology that is below her knowledge level will make her distrust the source. She expects the conversation to happen at her level.
- "I torture myself" — Why this phrase matters: This phrase reveals the self-punishing loop she is trapped in. She doesn't just have skin concerns — she actively makes herself suffer by examining, comparing, and re-examining. Copy that names this loop — and offers a way out of it — will hit differently than copy that just promises results.
- "feeling hopeless" — Why this phrase matters: CORE INSIGHT — this is the emotional destination of years of failed attempts. Not just frustrated. Hopeless. Any solution that speaks to the worn-out, tried-everything woman rather than the optimistic first-time buyer will land with disproportionate power.
