# Puffy Face — Avatar Deep Dive Research

## Pain Points
- "I've struggled with acne throughout most of my 20s and while the acne and regular breakouts are behind me, the texture of my skin bothers me. I feel like I have scarring left behind and that I have to use a bunch of acids and expensive treatments to get the smooth and even texture and complexion that I want."
Insight: Even after surviving the acne battle, the war isn't over. The residual damage — scars, texture, uneven tone — becomes a new, equally demoralizing enemy. This person doesn't feel "fixed." They feel like they traded one problem for another, and now need to invest even MORE time and money to clean up the aftermath.
- "I've struggled to achieve clear, glass skin without blackheads and pimples, and this is the routine that actually worked for me. The most important step that was holding me back from glass skin was touching my face and squeezing blackheads. I used to do it constantly because I have bad eyesight (I'm nearsighted), so if I wasn't wearing glasses or contacts, I saw my face better up close."
Insight: The pain point here is compulsive behavior driven by hyperfocus on perceived flaws. They literally re-engineered their daily habits (wearing contacts to avoid mirror magnification) just to stop themselves from making their skin worse. This reveals the psychological torment of being unable to leave the skin alone — a self-destructive loop driven by obsession over imperfection.
- "I have bad blood circulation, when I was 16 I already got Varicose Veins and spider veins. Also horrible looking cellulite even though I was skinny and working out. Also water in my legs. I envy women with beautiful legs so much."
Insight: For some, the pain isn't about products or routines — it's biological and feels utterly beyond their control. The phrase "I envy women with beautiful legs so much" reveals the deep emotional wound of watching others have naturally what you're physically incapable of achieving. This is grief, not just frustration.
- "I don't understand it, up until like 14, always had perfect skin, my skin isn't too bad now but compared to my family it definitely has imperfections, have problems with hyperpigmentation, acne, oily skin, dark circles around eyes."
Insight: The pain is amplified by the memory of "before." This person isn't just dissatisfied with their skin — they're mourning the version of themselves they used to be. Comparing themselves to family members adds social pressure and makes the regression feel personal and shameful.
- "i feel like strawberry... still need help with the texture tho that's one of the big icks about my skin."
Insight: The word "strawberry" and "big icks" reveal visceral disgust toward their own skin. This isn't mild dissatisfaction — it's repulsion. The avatar has internalized their skin's appearance as something objectively unpleasant, not just imperfect.
- "with the acne marks it was hard to accept my own self."
Insight: CORE INSIGHT. This is the naked emotional truth beneath all the product questions. Skin issues aren't cosmetic inconveniences — they're identity threats. The inability to accept one's own face is a profound psychological wound that drives every product search, every Reddit post, every desperate try of a new routine.
- "I've tried a few essences and toners but nothing seems to hit right."
Insight: This is the exhaustion of repeated disappointment. They've done the work. They've spent the money. And they still feel like they're failing. The phrase "nothing seems to hit right" captures the demoralizing experience of effort without reward.

## Failures
- "I've tried a few essences and toners but nothing seems to hit right."
Insight: Vague, repeated product experimentation without a systematic approach leads to a dead end. The failure isn't one product — it's the whole trial-and-error method. They've spent money and time and still feel no closer. This is the failure of randomness dressed up as effort.
- "I tried COSRX, Beauty of Joseon, Anua and skin1004. I feel like all of them lightened my face :/ But my skin is moisturized."
Insight: Even popular, well-reviewed products can create unintended consequences — in this case, melanin stripping that wasn't wanted. The resigned "But my skin is moisturized" is a bittersweet acceptance of a partial win wrapped in a real loss. The failure here is trusting community consensus without accounting for her individual skin response and complexion concerns.
- "Having wasted years in experimenting and falling for marketing gimmicks, found a good dermat who understands me and is competent."
Insight: CORE INSIGHT. "Years" and "marketing gimmicks" — this is a person who has been burned repeatedly by the beauty industry's promises. The real failure wasn't any single product; it was the entire paradigm of DIY trial-and-error. Their ultimate fix came from professional guidance, not Reddit rabbit holes or influencer recommendations.
- "I used to remove makeup with oils instead of micellar water, but I don't think oils clean as thoroughly."
Insight: A failure of incomplete information. This person did what felt right (oils feel luxurious, moisturizing) but it was actually undermining their skin goals. The failure here is that "feels good" doesn't always equal "works well" — and the skincare industry often conflates the two.
- "I did benzoyl peroxide as a spot treatment at first, but while it did quickly zap away at acne it also made them scar awfully bad which would take forever to fade."
Insight: Solving one problem while creating another is a deeply frustrating failure pattern. The person fixed their breakout but inherited a worse, slower-to-resolve problem: scarring. This failure teaches that aggressive short-term solutions often create long-term damage — and it breeds skepticism toward any "quick fix."
- "I started about 4 weeks ago with 0.025% breaking out like crazy."
Insight: The purging phase of tretinoin is a well-documented failure point. Many people start, experience an apparent worsening of their skin, and quit — never reaching the results on the other side. This is the failure of lack of context: without knowing that "it gets worse before it gets better," the experience feels like proof that the product simply doesn't work for you.
- "I've used both Retinol and Tretinoin and can't say I've seen the kind of results people rave about."
Insight: When celebrated holy grail products fail to deliver expected results, the avatar experiences a compounded disappointment. Not only did the product not work — it undermines trust in the entire community's recommendations. "People rave about" reveals the pressure of social proof that sets expectations too high.

Insight: The goal isn't just aesthetics — it's hope. The question "Is this achievable?" is really asking: "Can someone like me, with my history, with my damage, actually get there?" They're not just looking for a product recommendation. They're looking for permission to believe.
- "How can I have smoother looking skin? I'm particularly trying to reduce my forehead wrinkles, eye wrinkles/bags, and midline cheeks."
Insight: The goal is highly specific and anatomically mapped. This isn't vague dissatisfaction — they know exactly what bothers them and exactly where. The specificity signals that they've spent significant time studying their own face, probably in close-up mirrors or camera self-analysis. The goal is targeted self-improvement, not wholesale transformation.
- "What can I do to eliminate large pores and texture?"
Insight: The word "eliminate" reveals a maximalist goal — not "reduce" or "minimize" but completely remove. This mirrors the glass skin ideal itself: zero imperfection. The avatar isn't seeking improvement; they're seeking eradication of the problem. Understanding this helps position any product not as "help" but as a path to total resolution.

Insight: Durability is a hidden goal layer. It's not just about achieving the look — it's about maintaining it through real life: long hours, sweat, daily activity. The glass skin goal isn't a morning selfie achievement; it's an all-day presence goal. Products that deliver this become worth premium pricing.

Insight: This is the goal articulated as a destination with milestones. "Getting close" signals active progress tracking. Each one tackled is a step toward the unified ideal.

Insight: CORE INSIGHT. A significant portion of the avatar population holds the belief that glass skin is largely manufactured — filters, lighting, editing. This creates internal cognitive dissonance: they simultaneously want it AND suspect it doesn't exist. Any product or routine that promises glass skin must first dismantle this skepticism before it can sell hope.
- "social media has really caused some serious skin dysmorphia."
Insight: Some users have developed enough self-awareness to name the psychological mechanism behind their obsession. This is a sophisticated belief that the problem isn't their skin — it's their perception of it, distorted by curated digital imagery. Yet even holding this belief, they still pursue the ideal. The belief doesn't neutralize the desire.
- "Glass skin looks better online than in person. In person, it can make a person look oily or sweaty."
Insight: There's a growing belief that the aesthetic itself may be unattractive in real life — that the influencer look translates poorly to three-dimensional human interaction. This is a defensive belief that protects against disappointment while subtly undermining motivation to pursue the goal.
- "though genetics are involved, still everyone mostly has the genetic capability to achieve such skin."
Insight: CORE INSIGHT. A hopeful, democratizing belief — that glass skin is not exclusively the domain of the genetically gifted. This belief is the psychological foundation that keeps people trying. Without it, there's no reason to buy a single product. Copywriting that reinforces this belief ("your genetics aren't the ceiling — your routine is") will resonate powerfully.
- "Expensive products don't make a huge difference."
Insight: The avatar widely believes that price is not a reliable proxy for effectiveness. This creates both an opportunity (affordable products can win their loyalty) and a challenge (premium products must justify their cost with visible, real results, not marketing). - "I always prefer getting a custom plan made with my dermatologist. Having wasted years in experimenting and falling for marketing gimmicks."
Insight: Growing belief that professional guidance beats community experimentation. This belief, when activated, moves the avatar away from product purchasing and toward clinical consultation. Brands that acknowledge the limitations of DIY while positioning themselves as dermatologist-aligned or scientifically validated will earn more trust from this segment.

## Objections
- "The purging phase of tretinoin is real — skins more oily and I've broken out a bit more."
Insight: "It gets worse before it gets better" is one of the most powerful objections to starting any active ingredient. If a product makes someone's skin look and feel worse in the first weeks, most will abandon it before seeing results. Overcoming this objection requires pre-emptive education and expectation-setting.
- "I'm worried that it won't be as effective as one that's 15% (what the studies done to show its benefits were based on)."
Insight: Ingredient concentration skepticism is a real objection from the more research-literate segment of the avatar. They're not just buying products — they're evaluating efficacy benchmarks. A product needs to justify its formula with data, not just claims.
- "Glass skin is not real. Just social media came with this... Healthy skin, clear skin maybe! Skin will skinning sometimes."
Insight: CORE INSIGHT. The belief that glass skin is a fabricated standard creates an objection to the entire category. If the goal itself is fake, why invest? This objection requires reframing — positioning the product not as a path to "glass skin" (a filtered aesthetic) but as a path to "your healthiest, most radiant skin" (a real, personal achievement).
- "I'm not a huge fan of clicking pics of my own self and back then had no confidence for clicking one either."
Insight: Self-consciousness so deep it prevents before-and-after documentation. This objection isn't about the product — it's about emotional readiness and self-acceptance. It reveals that the avatar may be too ashamed of their current state to engage fully with transformation journeys. Brands need to create safe, non-judgmental entry points.
- "It scares me... knowing people had a positive experience is reassuring."
Insight: Fear of side effects, adverse reactions, and medical risk is a persistent objection — especially with prescription actives like tretinoin or accutane. Reassurance through real user testimonials from people with similar concerns is the most effective counter. Clinical authority alone is insufficient; peer experience matters enormously.
- "I tried Korean skincare 100%... I feel like all of them lightened my face."
Insight: Fear of unintended skin tone alteration — particularly among users of color — is a significant objection to adopting popular products. This concern is emotionally loaded ("I don't want my melanin to be stripped") and requires specific reassurance around ingredient transparency and inclusivity.
- "Glass skin looks kinda cool in pictures but in real life it just looks kinda greasy lol."
Insight: The objection that the aesthetic itself may not translate to real life undermines motivation. If the end result is "greasy-looking" rather than glowing, the entire pursuit feels misguided. Products need to promise and deliver a specific visual quality — dewy and luminous, not oily and wet — and the distinction must be made explicit.

## Level of Awareness
**Level: Problem Aware → Solution Aware (transitioning)**
The majority of this avatar sits squarely in Problem Aware territory — they know their skin has issues (texture, pores, hyperpigmentation, dullness, scarring), they feel the pain of those issues daily, and they're actively looking for answers. However, a significant portion has moved into Solution Aware — they know that categories of solutions exist (Korean skincare, tretinoin, chemical exfoliants, dermatologist procedures) but haven't found THE product or routine that works for them specifically.
- "I've been trying to get that smooth, glowing finish everyone talks about, but my skin always ends up looking oily instead of dewy. I've tried a few essences and toners but nothing seems to hit right."
Insight: Classic Solution Aware positioning. They know solutions exist (essences, toners), they've tried multiple, and none have solved the problem. They're not asking "what IS glass skin" — they're asking "why can't I GET it?" This person needs specificity, not category education.
- "I don't use Korean skincare currently, but I'm looking to get into it."
Insight: This is Problem Aware moving toward Solution Aware. They know the solution category exists (K-beauty), they haven't yet adopted it, and they're at the consideration threshold. This is a perfect entry-point moment for product education and category onboarding.
- "Tretinoin and sunscreen are a great start" / "TRETINOIN" / "Dermat prescribed Tretinoin. Go to dermat and ask for it."
Insight: The community has educated itself to a degree that some members are effectively Product Aware — they know specific products (tretinoin, snail mucin, niacinamide) and are evaluating and recommending them. But the original posters in most threads are still earlier in the awareness cycle, looking for product discovery rather than product comparison.

## Real Customer Language
- "glass skin" — Why this phrase matters: This is the aspirational shorthand for a complete transformation — smooth, luminous, pore-free, glowing from within. It's the category-defining term that organizes all desire, all searching, all purchasing. But critically, it's also contested — half the community believes it's real, half believes it's filtered fiction. Any brand using this phrase must either own the myth or reframe it as achievable reality.
- "I feel like I lost my glow" — Why this phrase matters: "Glow" is the emotional currency of this space. Losing it isn't just a skin change — it's a personal regression. This phrase is perfect copy language because it activates identity, not just aesthetics. "Get your glow back" is more emotionally powerful than "achieve glass skin" because it speaks to restoration, not impossible idealism.
- "my skin always ends up looking oily instead of dewy" — Why this phrase matters: The oily/dewy distinction is one of the avatar's most consistent and frustrating daily experiences. It shows sophisticated vocabulary AND real pain. Copy that speaks directly to this tension ("the line between oily and dewy") will cut through immediately because it proves you understand the specific failure they keep hitting.
- "it was hard to accept my own self" — Why this phrase matters: This is the emotional bedrock of the entire category. Behind every product search is this sentence. Any brand that can acknowledge this truth — not exploit it, but acknowledge it — will earn a loyalty that goes far beyond skincare. This language reveals that what's being sold is not product; it's self-acceptance.
- "I feel like strawberry" — Why this phrase matters: Hyper-specific, sensory, self-deprecating but devastating language for skin texture. The imagery is instantly relatable to anyone with pores and bumpy texture. Using this kind of visceral, specific language in copy creates immediate recognition — "they're talking about MY skin" — which is the first step to trust.
- "consistency is key" / "patience, a hell lot of it" — Why this phrase matters: CORE INSIGHT. This phrase appears in multiple posts in different forms. It's the community's hard-won wisdom — and it's also their most common frustration. People KNOW consistency matters; they struggle to maintain it. Copy that acknowledges the discipline required (and supports it rather than promising overnight miracles) will feel honest and trustworthy.
- "purging phase" — Why this phrase matters: This is technical vocabulary that the Solution Aware avatar uses fluently. It signals they've been burned by products that "worked" by looking like they failed first. Any communication about actives (tretinoin, acids, retinoids) must address this phase explicitly, or users will abandon the product at the exact moment it's about to deliver.
- "dropping the routine" / "less is more" — Why this phrase matters: A recurring theme of skepticism toward complexity. After years of multi-step routines not delivering, many users are cycling back to simplicity. Copy that validates a streamlined approach — "you don't need 12 steps, you need the right 3" — speaks directly to this fatigue and positions the product as the intelligent, edited choice.

## Trigger Moments
- "I feel like I lost my glow. I switched from the clear blue cerave moisturizer bottle to the white bottle with the green on it? Should I drop cerave completely?"
Insight: A product change that worsens skin is a powerful trigger. When something they trusted is disrupted — a reformulation, a substitution, a new product that doesn't work — they immediately go searching for answers and alternatives. The moment of loss creates the urgency to find a solution. This is a high-intent shopping moment disguised as a question.
- "I've struggled with acne throughout most of my 20s and while the acne and regular breakouts are behind me, the texture of my skin bothers me."
Insight: The transition from active acne to post-acne aftermath is a major trigger moment. Clearing the acne doesn't bring the satisfaction they expected — instead, they discover a new problem (scarring, texture, hyperpigmentation) that reopens the wound. This life event — "I fixed one thing and now I see the damage it left behind" — sends people into active solution-seeking mode with real purchasing intent.
- "I don't understand it, up until like 14, always had perfect skin, my skin isn't too bad now but compared to my family it definitely has imperfections."
Insight: Comparing oneself to family — people who share genetics but have different skin outcomes — is a trigger that combines envy, confusion, and a sense of unfairness. "Why does my sibling have clear skin and I don't?" is an unanswerable question that creates emotional urgency to DO something. Family gatherings, photos, social comparison events are recurring triggers for this avatar's search.
- "I went home to visit family a month later and everyone was commenting on my skin."
