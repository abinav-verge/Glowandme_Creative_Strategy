## Pain Points
- "I hate how my face looks."
Insight: This is not cosmetic vanity — this is a woman in pain over her own reflection. The brevity of this statement carries enormous emotional weight. She's not exaggerating. She's exhausted.
- "It's been a painful experience to watch my looks change so much that I don't recognise myself."
Insight: Identity disruption is at the core of this pain. The face is how we present ourselves to the world. When it changes without permission or explanation, the psychological damage goes far deeper than aesthetics — she is mourning her former self.
- "I don't even recognize myself in the mirror anymore, my face is so different."
Insight: CORE INSIGHT — Multiple people express this exact sentiment in different words. Loss of self-recognition is a recurring, devastating experience. This is not about looking "less attractive" — it's about feeling like a stranger in one's own body.
- "It's been greatly affecting my body image and it's also uncomfortable, sometimes even painful. The swelling around my neck and throat gets so bad I have problems swallowing and talking and my throat is constantly sore like I have a head cold. It feels like a lump in my throat or something squeezing around my neck."
Insight: The pain is not only psychological — it's viscerally physical. Difficulty swallowing and talking means this condition is invading basic daily functioning. This person is not complaining about vanity. She is describing something that impairs her quality of life at a fundamental level.
- "Family members have been making harsh comments, it's really getting to me tbh."
Insight: Social cruelty compounds the medical suffering. When the people who are supposed to love you unconditionally comment on your appearance, the wound goes beyond embarrassment — it becomes shame. This is a trigger point that makes the desire to fix this urgent and emotionally charged.
- "I even get depressed, for two years I've been trying to figure out how to cure this with no answers."
Insight: Two years of searching with no resolution. The depression here isn't separate from the puffiness — it IS the puffiness, plus the helplessness, plus the medical dismissal, all compounding into an invisible, chronic despair.
- "My face is very puffy, my neck is sore, my joints hurt, hair shedding increase and bloating."
Insight: The puffiness does not exist in isolation. It is part of a cascading, whole-body inflammatory experience that makes every day feel like a battle on multiple fronts simultaneously.
